My first "proper" job was a fixed term graduate role at a whisky startup. I spent three months flying between Edinburgh and London, sitting in on brand strategy meetings with people who'd been in the industry for decades, learning how to think about positioning and differentiation. I absolutely loved it though.

After that I ended up at a small audio tech company in a country where I didn't speak the language, starting as an intern and eventually running the marketing function. Over a few years, I built a specific kind of expertise — I knew how to position a technical product, how to build a content engine from nothing, and how to pivot a company's entire growth strategy when the market shifted.

That knowledge was my moat. It's what got me hired at Whimsical, what made me useful to clients as a consultant, and what I built my professional identity around.

---

### The moat erodes

Today, this type of knowledge is less valuable.

Not worthless — understanding how users think, how markets work, and how positioning shapes growth still matters. But the tactical execution layer that used to take years to learn can now be compressed into weeks. Tools like Claude can generate positioning frameworks, write decent copy, and synthesize user research faster than I ever could manually.

The shift didn't happen overnight. It was more like watching a tide come in. Each new capability was incremental, but the cumulative effect was clear: the things I spent years learning to do were becoming commoditised.

I noticed it first with clients. The questions they used to need a consultant for — "How should we position this?" or "What should our onboarding emails say?" — they were increasingly answering themselves with AI assistance. Not perfectly, but well enough that the bar for outside help was rising fast.

---

### First attempt

My initial response was to try and stay ahead. Take courses, learn new tools, add more layers of knowledge. I went through Reforge's Growth Series and Product Marketing Series. I started learning PostHog and Mixpanel in more depth. I thought the answer was more knowledge, applied faster.

It helped, but it was treating symptoms. The fundamental problem wasn't that I needed to know more — it was that knowing things was becoming less of an advantage.

At the same time, I was using Claude more and more for my own work. At first it was just a faster way to write. Then it became a research tool. Then a thinking partner. Then something I couldn't really work without.

---

### The change

The real shift happened when I co-founded Pulss, a brand analytics startup.

For the first time, I wasn't doing marketing for someone else's product. I was trying to build the product itself. I needed user authentication, a database, a way to display survey data, API integrations — and I had no engineering background.

What I had was Claude and a willingness to be bad at things.

The first few weeks were humbling. I'd ask Claude to build something, get code back, have no idea if it was good, paste it in anyway, watch it break, and then spend an hour figuring out what went wrong. But each time, I understood a little more. Not enough to write code from scratch, but enough to know what questions to ask and what the answers should roughly look like.

It felt like I'd unlocked skills I didn't have before. Not in a magical way — more like having a translator who's extremely patient with your terrible pronunciation. The knowledge was still on the other side of a gap. But the gap was crossable now.

---

### Learning to think differently

The biggest lesson wasn't about AI tools or coding. It was about how to approach problems when your old toolkit doesn't apply.

Before, when I faced a new challenge, I'd look for frameworks, best practices, established approaches. That's what consultants do — you apply known solutions to known problems. It works well when the solution space is mapped.

Building with AI is different. The solution space is unmapped. Nobody has fifteen years of experience doing this. The best practices don't exist yet, or they change every few months. The people who are good at it are good at it because they're willing to iterate, to be wrong, and to learn in public.

Knowledge may not be as valuable, but it definitely still pays to know enough to go with the flow. Understanding what an API does, even if you can't write one from scratch, matters. Knowing how databases work, even if you've never configured one manually, matters. The knowledge becomes context for better prompts, better architecture decisions, and better problem decomposition.

---

### Starting with the hard part

I spent a long time putting off building a personal site because I wasn't sure what to say about myself. The marketing person in me wanted clean positioning. A crisp one-liner. A defined niche.

But the truth is messier. I'm a marketing person who became a product person who is now learning to build things. I've consulted for SaaS companies and co-founded a startup and written production code and I'm still not entirely sure which of those things defines what I do next.

This site is an attempt to just put the work out there and see what resonates. No clean positioning — just the things I've done, the things I've learned, and what I'm building now.

If that sounds like someone who could be useful to what you're working on, I'd like to hear about it.
